The Taj Mahal

The feelings and responses evoked by the world's greatest buildings are as diverse as the buildings
The Egyptian pyramids and the Great wall of China stun the imagination with their sheer scale, one is awed
by the pitiless power implied in the ability to organize such massive undertakings, a Gothic Cathedral
impresses us with intense faith that motivated its builders. The Parthenon, by contrast, expresses the Greek
love of rationality and proportion.

The Taj Mahal, the superb mausoleum built by seventeenth century Mogul emperor of India, Shah Jehan, for his
Queen, Mumtaz Mahal, seems to have all these qualities, but above all it displays the feelings of love and
romance in its purest sense.

The Taj Mahal, stand in an enormous rectangular garden, enclosed by a wall of sandstone, at the other end of
the garden is the fine gateway, also made of sandstone and inlaid with ornaments and Koranic calligraphy
on white marble. A perfectly symmetrical building, the Taj Mahal sits on the end of a large marble pool
perfectly positioned to reflect the tomb.

Millions upon millions of tourist visit this symbolic structure every year, in Agra, India. One of the
wonders of the mordern world it stands there patiently, perfectly, majestically. Withstanding the test of time,
as it has been since 1631.
